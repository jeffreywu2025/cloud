Unit 2 Formative Discussion: ROCCA and ToGAF

Review the Anggraini N., Binariswanto and Legowo, N. 2019 article and post a discussion about ROCCA and ToGAF implementation in Government Agencies.


Anggraini, Binariswanto and Legowo (2019) propose an integrated framework combining the Roadmap for Cloud Computing Adoption (ROCCA) and TOGAF 9.2 to address a persistent challenge in public sector IT transformation: the misalignment between organisational strategy and technological implementation. Their central contribution lies not merely in applying two established models, but in mapping ROCCA’s five operational phases (Analysis, Planning, Adoption, Migration and Management) to the TOGAF Architecture Development Method (ADM), thereby embedding cloud adoption within a formal enterprise architecture governance structure.

Critically, ROCCA provides procedural clarity through structured readiness assessment, compliance evaluation and staged migration planning. However, in isolation it lacks architectural depth and traceability mechanisms. TOGAF compensates for this limitation by introducing formal artefacts—such as Architecture Vision, Business Capability Maps and Migration Roadmaps—which enhance strategic coherence and documentation discipline. The mapping presented in the study demonstrates that TOGAF particularly strengthens the Analysis and Planning phases, reinforcing business–IT alignment theory by ensuring that cloud decisions are derived from articulated organisational capabilities rather than ad hoc technical drivers.

Nevertheless, the framework is not without limitations. Its reliance on TOGAF may introduce significant methodological overhead, potentially making it resource-intensive for smaller or less mature government agencies. Moreover, validation through a single case study (SKK Migas) constrains generalisability. The evaluation is largely based on managerial judgement rather than measurable performance indicators such as cost efficiency, service quality improvement or risk reduction. From an institutional perspective, the cautious hybrid-cloud strategy adopted reflects public sector risk aversion and regulatory pressure rather than purely strategic optimisation (Chang and Johnston, 2019).

Recent scholarship reinforces both the value and constraints of such governance-driven models. Lnenicka and Komarkova (2019) emphasise the importance of enterprise architecture in managing digital ecosystems, while Alenezi and Basu (2022) and Osei-Bryson and Ngwenyama (2023) highlight governance, interoperability and risk management as decisive success factors. However, emerging research also stresses agility and adaptive governance—dimensions less developed in the ROCCA–TOGAF integration.

In conclusion, the integrated framework represents a structurally robust and governance-oriented approach to public sector cloud adoption. Its strength lies in strategic alignment and compliance assurance; however, future research should test its scalability, agility and measurable impact across diverse governmental contexts.
